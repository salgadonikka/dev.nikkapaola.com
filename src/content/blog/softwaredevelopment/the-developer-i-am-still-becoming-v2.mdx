I knew I wanted to work with computers before I even understood what that meant.

My prep school yearbook says *computer analyst* next to my name. I wrote it because that was my aunt's job, the one that eventually took her to America. I had no idea what a computer analyst actually did. It just sounded like the right direction.

The real moment came in third year high school, in my very first programming class. We were learning Pascal. Most of my classmates were miserable. I was having the time of my life. Fourth year, Visual Basic, same story. I'd sit down, get into it, and feel something click. That was when the quiet thought started forming: *maybe this is what I'm supposed to do.*

College confirmed it in unexpected ways. I took up Computer Engineering thinking I'd finally be surrounded by people like me. Turns out, most of my classmates weren't particularly into it. I remember a C# class where the professor asked how to exit a `for` loop early. Silence. I knew the answer but I was shy, so I stayed quiet. Then the professor started offering extra points on the exam. The number kept climbing. At some point I did the math, raised my hand, and answered.

I still think about that moment. Not because of the points, but because of how it felt. Like a small, quiet confirmation that this was mine.

There was also the time I coded a whole voting system overnight because my partner in a pair project never showed up. I watched the sun go down and come back up. Took a shower, went to school, presented it. My partner got the same grade. I wasn't upset. I just felt proud.

I think about those early years as the easy ones. Not because they were simple, but because the feelings were so clear: curiosity, excitement, the uncomplicated joy of building something and watching it work.

And then I started working. And that's when it got interesting.

---

## When you sign your name on a dotted line

At some point in my career, I found myself locked into a role I hadn't chosen for myself.

It started fine. I had just come off a strong start, felt confident, felt recognized. Then that recognition led to a reassignment to the most demanding project the organization had. Mostly system analysis. Almost no actual programming. I was unhappy.

And I had signed a bond.

If you've never been in a bonded role, here's what it feels like: you're not stuck, exactly. You're just... committed. Legally. For years. You come in every morning and do work you didn't choose, in a capacity that doesn't quite fit you, because the alternative is worse. You can't leave. So you learn to wait.

> The bond eventually ends. What you do in the meantime is the part that matters, and it took me too long to figure that out.

What I wish I had done during that time was stay curious. Keep building things on the side, keep learning. What I actually did was survive it. And while I did eventually leave and land on my feet, I came out of it not as sharp as I could have been.

If you're in a bonded role right now and you're not happy, here is the only advice I can give: keep the skills warm. Don't let the comfort of a guaranteed paycheck and a countdown clock convince you that you can pause.

You can't pause. You can only fall behind.

## What a toxic environment actually costs you

At another point, I worked somewhere with a genuinely difficult culture.

I don't want to dwell on the specifics because honestly, the specifics don't matter. What matters is the pattern, and I suspect it's one a lot of developers will recognize. The credit going to people who hadn't done the work. The manager who led through intimidation instead of trust. The colleagues who stayed quiet to keep the attention off themselves. The slow, grinding sense that no matter how good your output was, the game was rigged in other ways.

I did good work there. I actually grew a lot technically. I got to build something from the ground up, propose ideas, explore parts of the stack I hadn't touched before. On paper, it looked like a great chapter.

But the environment was eating at me in ways I didn't fully notice until I was already worn down.

> Technical growth doesn't cancel out the cost of a bad environment. I learned this the hard way.

Here's what a toxic workplace actually costs you, beyond the obvious: it trains you to protect yourself instead of take risks. You stop proposing things because proposals get stolen. You stop asking questions because questions invite criticism. You start measuring your energy carefully, rationing it, because the environment keeps draining it faster than you can refill.

By the time I left, I was a better developer technically but a more cautious person. And cautious people don't always grow as fast as they should.

---

## The trap of a comfortable, well-run place

This one is harder to talk about because it doesn't look like a problem from the outside.

I've worked somewhere with great culture, real Scrum, mature pipelines, fantastic teammates. A place where the infrastructure was so solid that I could focus entirely on the code. Where the process actually worked the way it was supposed to. Where I liked the people I worked with.

And I stopped growing.

Not because I was lazy. Not because I wasn't doing my job. But because the environment was so stable, and my role within it so defined, that there was no friction. No pressure to stretch. No reason to look beyond the stack we were already using.

I kept waiting to get exposure to newer projects, newer technologies, the things I could see other teams working on. The waiting became the work. And while I was waiting, the tech world kept moving.

◆ Note

A comfortable environment is not the same as a growth environment. Sometimes the most dangerous place to be in your career is somewhere everything is working fine.

There's a version of this that's your fault, and a version that's structural. Mine was a bit of both. The opportunity wasn't there, but I also wasn't creating opportunity. I was waiting to be handed something I should have been building myself.

## Now it's 2026

I'm sitting here freshening up on things I used to know by feel. Updating a resume and having a hard time knowing what to put in it. Looking at a tech landscape that has changed enormously and feeling the gap between where I am and where I want to be.

I have a lot of feelings about that. Regret, mostly. That familiar tightness of *I should have done more with the time I had.*

But here's the thing I keep coming back to.

The good times were good. But maybe their real purpose wasn't just to feel good. Maybe they were supposed to make me feel capable enough to want more. And the hard times, the bonds, the toxic environments, the comfortable plateaus, maybe those weren't just obstacles. Maybe they were supposed to push me somewhere. To open my eyes to what was always there, waiting to be noticed.

> It's not too late. It just feels like it sometimes, and that feeling is lying.

I'm building my own apps now. Putting together a portfolio. Showing up on the internet in a way I haven't before. In the era of .NET 10, coming from someone whose working knowledge is mostly .NET 4.8, that gap is real. But I'm not pretending it isn't there. I'm just deciding what to do about it.

One year from now, I want to read this post and feel something different. Not the sting of *I should have started sooner.* Something closer to: *I said I would. And I did.*
