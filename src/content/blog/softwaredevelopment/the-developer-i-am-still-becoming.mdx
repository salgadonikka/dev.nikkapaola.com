I knew I wanted to work with computers before I even really understood what that meant.

My prep school yearbook says *computer analyst* under my name. I wrote it because that was my aunt's job, the one that eventually brought her to America. And back then, moving to America was the dream. I had no idea what a computer analyst actually did. But I figured if that's what got you there, then that's what I wanted to be.

The real story started in third year high school, when I had my very first programming class. We were learning Pascal. My classmates were losing their minds over it. Low grades, complaints everywhere, general chaos directed at our teacher. I remember thinking, *what are they so upset about?* I was having a great time. I actually liked her. She became my favorite teacher. She was strict, sure, which was why everyone called her "the police." But I thought she was great.

Fourth year came and it was Visual Basic. Same story. I'd sit down and just... get into it. Something clicked every time. That was when the thought started forming quietly in the background: *maybe this is what I'm supposed to do.*

---

## The Shy Girl Who Knew the Answer

College was a bit of a surprise. I took up Computer Engineering thinking I was finally going to be surrounded by people like me, people who genuinely loved this stuff. Turns out, most of my classmates were just there. Programming wasn't really their thing.

There's one memory I keep coming back to. We were in a C# class and the professor asked how you break out of a `for` loop early. Silence. Nobody answered. I knew. I absolutely knew. But I was shy and I kept my mouth shut.

Then the professor started offering extra points on the exam. A few points. Then more. Then more. At some point, I did the math and thought, *okay, I cannot let this go.* So I raised my hand and answered.

I got the points. More importantly, I remember how that felt. Like a quiet little confirmation that this was mine.

Then there was the voting system.

I had a pair programming project and my partner basically never showed up to actually do anything. So I stayed up the whole night and coded the entire thing myself. I watched the sun go down. I watched it come back up. Took a shower, went to school, presented it, defended it. My partner got free grades. I should have been upset about that. Honestly, I wasn't. I just felt proud.

That was around 2009 or 2010. Looking back now, I think of those as the easy years. Not because they were simple, but because everything I felt was so clear. Curiosity, excitement, fulfillment. The uncomplicated joy of building something and watching it actually work.

---

## The First Job, and the Feeling of Being Good

My first real job was at a bank in the Philippines, and it started with a bootcamp. Three months of classroom training, three months rotating through different teams, then six months of actual deployment. And at the end of it, you present a project. Pass and get promoted. Fail and go home. Out of 25 people, I finished in the top 5.

I felt great. I felt like I had figured it out. Like I just had to keep doing what I was doing and everything would be fine.

That feeling, as it turns out, was the beginning of my problem.

Being recognized for my work got me reassigned to the biggest, hardest project the bank had: migrating from a legacy core banking system to a new one. It sounds impressive. It was not what I wanted. The work was mostly system analysis. Almost no actual programming. I was unhappy, and I had signed a three-year bond, so I didn't have much of a choice.

What I also didn't have, and didn't realize I was missing, was any awareness of what was happening outside the walls of my office. Cloud infrastructure was being built. DevOps was becoming a whole discipline. The tech world was evolving at a speed I wasn't paying attention to. I was comfortable, recognized, doing my job well, and completely unaware that the world was quietly moving on without me.

---

## The Late Night Studying and the Bloodshot Eyes

Before my bond ended, I knew I had to move. So I started studying until 2am. Refreshing .NET basics. Doing interview prep. Going back to fundamentals I should have kept sharp but hadn't touched in a while.

I remember one interview that went for an hour or two and was brutal in the best possible way. When it was over, I looked in the mirror and my eyes were completely red. But I got the offer. I moved the moment the bond was done.

This company was different. I was part of an R&D team building an in-house test automation tool. A real product, built from the ground up, with room to explore. I got to work with WPF, Selenium, design patterns, MVC, JavaScript. I even proposed my own project and ran with it. I was ambitious. I was learning again. I felt like myself.

But the environment was rough. Office politics, credit going to people who hadn't done the work, a manager who led through intimidation, teammates who kept their heads down to stay safe. The stress got to me in a real way. I eventually started looking for something else not because I'd stopped growing technically, but because the toll on me personally was too high.

---

## The Best Culture, The Smallest Ceiling

The next company had great culture. Real Scrum, international scope, teammates on the other side of the world. A mature pipeline where I could focus on the code, follow the process, push to the server and trust that everything would run correctly. That part was genuinely satisfying.

The work was also challenging in a good way. The system was huge and I was constantly working to understand different parts of it. But the technology never expanded. Same stack, same framework, every project. There was a team in the same organization working in React on something new and exciting and I kept waiting to get pulled in. It never happened.

Then the pandemic came. And somewhere in the middle of it, so did a personal health battle that shifted everything for me.

I made a choice during that period to slow down and focus on myself. I don't regret that entirely. There were things I needed to learn that had nothing to do with a tech stack. But I also let my skills sit still. I watched the industry accelerate and told myself I'd catch up when things settled down. The problem is, things never really settle down in tech.

---

## Now It's 2026

It's the era of the cloud. AI is not a buzzword anymore, it's just... infrastructure. Everything is moving so fast it's almost funny.

I'm sitting here freshening up on .NET basics and struggling to explain things I used to know by feel. I'm updating my resume and having a hard time knowing what to even put in it. I don't have a portfolio full of personal projects yet. I spent a long time building things for other people's companies and not much time building anything I could call my own.

I have a lot of feelings about that. Regret, yes. That familiar sense of urgency that makes your chest tight. The thought that keeps starting with *I should have...*

But here's what I'm trying to reframe, and I mean really trying, not just saying it because it sounds good.

Maybe the good times weren't supposed to just feel good. Maybe they were supposed to make you feel capable enough to want more. And maybe the difficult times, the bonds, the politics, the health scares, the long stretches of staying still, weren't supposed to just feel bad. Maybe they were supposed to push you somewhere. To open your eyes to what was always there, waiting to be noticed.

> It's not too late. It just feels like it sometimes, and that feeling is lying.

I am building my own apps now. I'm putting together a portfolio. I'm on this site, writing this post, showing up in a way I haven't before. In the time of .NET 10, coming from someone whose working knowledge is still mostly .NET 4.8, that's both embarrassing and, somehow, motivating. I didn't attempt to catch up for a long time. But I'm attempting now.

I may be late. I may feel a lot of regret about it, more often than I'd like to admit. But I really don't want to be writing this same thing a year from now.

So this is me committing. Not to a perfect timeline or a list of certifications or a five-year plan. Just to the version of me that actually does the thing instead of thinking about doing it.

One year from now, I want to read this post and feel something different. Not the sting of *I should have started sooner.* Something closer to: *I said I would. And I did.*
